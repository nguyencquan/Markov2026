\section{Eigenvalues of Stochastic Matrices}

\textbf{a)} Show that p has at least one eigenvalue equal to 1.

If p is a stochasitc matrix size NxN, then all of the rows sum up to 1. We are trying to find some matrix
v such that $pv = v$. Expanding this matrix multiplication we get a specific index of v being
$$
v_{j} = \sum_{i=1}^{N}p_{j,i}v_{i}
$$
However we know that $\sum_{i=1}^n p_{j,i}=1,\forall j$, hence if we define every vector in v as 1, then we have
$$
v_{j} = \sum_{i=1}^{N}p_{j,i} = 1
$$
Therefore, for any stochastic matrix, an eigenvalue of 1 will have a corresponding matrix with
a column length N being composed of 1, as all rows in the stochastic matrix sum to 1.

\textbf{b) Show that all the other eigenvalues have magnitudes less then or equal to 1.}

The Gershgorin circle theorem states that, if $R_i = \sum_{i\neq j}|a_{ij}|$, where i is some row
of the matrix A, then all eigenvalues of A exists in at least one gershgorin disk denoted by
$(D(a_{ii},R_i))$

Looking at a stochastic matrix which has all positive entries and all rows sum up to one, we know for all i
$$1 = \sum_{j=1}^Na_{ij} = a_{ii} + \sum_{i\neq j}a_{ij} = a_{ii}+\sum_{i\neq j}|a_{ij}|$$

Hence the maximum upper bound for this Gershgorin disk is 1, and is always 1.

Furthermore, since $0\leq a_{ii} \leq 1$ we can calculate the lower bound from

\begin{align*}
    &1-a_{ii} = R_i\\
    &\implies 0\leq R_i \leq 1\\
    &\implies 1 \geq a_{ii} - R_i \geq -1
\end{align*}

From the first part we see the largest possible upper bound is 1 and lowest lower bound is $-1$
hence the absolute value of all eigenvalues must be equal to or less then 1.

\textbf{c) Show that if p1 and p2 are stochastic matrices of the same size, p1p2 is also stochastic}

In order to be a stochastic matrix, the sum of rows must equal 1 hence if we look at a single row.
\begin{align*}
    1 = \sum_{j=1}^\infty p1p2_{i,j} &= \sum_{k=1}^N\sum_{j=1}^N p1_{i,j}p2_{j,k}\\
    &= \sum_{j=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^N p1_{i,j}p2_{j,k}\\
    &= \sum_{j=1}^Np1_{i,j}\sum_{k=1}^N p1_{j,k}\\
    &= \sum_{j=1}^Np1_{i,j}=1\\
\end{align*}
However this applies to all rows so the product of these two matrices are stochastic.

\textbf{c) show that $q_n$ converges exponentially to $\pi$}

Decomposing a starting distribution of everything being at 1 into eigenvectors, note I will
be using R however the values will be adjusted to match that of wolfram alpha...

$$
q_0^T = .3333v_1 + -0.3359v_2 + 0.0025v_3

However to make it easier to look at we will call this
$$
q_0^t = \hat{v_1}+\hat{v_2}+\hat{v_3}
$$

Now if we solve the next iteration:

$$
q_1^T = A(\hat{v_1}+\hat{v_2}+\hat{v_3}) = A\hat{v_1}+A\hat{v_2}+A\hat{v_3} = \hat{v_1}+\lambda_2\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3\hat{v_3}
$$
$$
q_2^T = A^2(\hat{v_1}+\hat{v_2}+\hat{v_3}) = \hat{v_1}+\lambda_2^2\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^2\hat{v_3}
$$

Hence $q_n$ must be
\begin{align*}
    q_n^T &= .3333v_1 +\lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3}\\
    &= \pi^T +\lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3}
\end{align*}

As with induction
\begin{align*}
    q_{n+1}^T =Aq_n^T &= A(\pi^T +\lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3})\\
    &= A\pi^T +\lambda_2^nA\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^nA\hat{v_3}\\
    &= \pi^T +\lambda_2^{n+1}\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^{n+1}\hat{v_3}
\end{align*}


However we know that as $n\rightarrow \infty$ and $|\lambda_2|,|\lambda_3|<1$ so let $K = max\{|\lambda_2|,|\lambda_3|\}$

$$
q_n^T - \pi^T= \lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3}
$$
$$
||q_n^T-\pi^T|| = ||\lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}+\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3}|| \leq ||\lambda_2^n\hat{v_2}||+||\lambda_3^n\hat{v_3}|| = |\lambda_2|^n ||\hat{v_2}|| + |\lambda_3|^n ||\hat{v_3}||
\leq K^n [||\hat{v_2}||+||\hat{v_3}||] \leq CK^n
$$

As a result the distribution must decay at least as fast as an exponential to the stationary distribution.

\section{Proof of Detailed balance for birth-death chains}

Given the stationary distribution, we end up with the equation:
$$\pi P = [\pi_0(1-p_0-q_0) + \pi_1q_1,\pi_0p_0+\pi_1(1-p_1-q_1) + \pi_2q_2,...,\pi_{N-1}p_{N-1} \pi_N(1-p_N-q_N)] = \pi$$
In other words, besides the boundary states we have

$$
\pi_i = \sum_{k=0}^N \pi_{k}p(k,i) = \pi_{i-1}p_{i-1}+\pi_i(1-p_i-q_i) + \pi_{i+1}q_{i+1}
$$

So starting with the intial case we have

$$P_0 = \pi_0(1-p_0-q_0) + \pi_1q_1$$

Assume the n'th case is true such that

$$P_n = P_n + q_{n+1}\pi_{n+1}-p_n\pi_n$$

Then we have
\begin{align*}
    P_{n+1} &= \sum_{j=0}^{n+1}\pi_j\\
    &=\pi_{n+1}+\sum_{j=0}^{n}\pi_j\\
    &= \pi_{n+1} + P_n\\
    &= \pi_{n+1}(1-p_{n+1} - q_{n+1}) + \pi_{n+2}q_{n+2} + \pi_{n}p_n + P_n\\
    &= \pi_{n+1}(1-p_{n+1} - q_{n+1}) + \pi_{n+2}q_{n+2} + \pi_{n}p_n + P_n + q_{n+1}\pi_{n+1}-p_n\pi_n\\
    &= \pi_{n+1}-\pi_{n+1}p_{n+1} + \pi_{n+2}q_{n+2} + P_n\\
    &=(P_n+\pi_{n+1})-\pi_{n+1}p_{n+1} + \pi_{n+2}q_{n+2}\\
    &=P_{n+1} + \pi_{n+2}q_{n+2}-\pi_{n+1}p_{n+1}\\
\end{align*}
